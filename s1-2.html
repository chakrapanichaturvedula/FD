Session 1.2 — Market Motives

Risk, Hedging
& Speculation

The same market can be used for completely different reasons. Discover the difference through scenarios — not definitions.

Classify market participants by their objective, not their instrument
Explain why certainty — not profit — is the hedger's goal
Understand that not hedging an existing exposure is itself a risk choice

Activity 1 · Sort the motive
Speculative risk or business exposure?

A trader buys Nifty expecting it to rise next week

An importer must pay dollars next month, worries the dollar may rise
Existing exposure — needs protection
Directional risk — taken for profit
Speculation means taking risk deliberately to profit from a market view. Hedging means reducing an existing risk that is already there from your business or position. The key question is always: is this person creating risk, or managing risk that already exists?
2
Activity 2 · Trader hats
Who is this — hedger or speculator?
Four scenarios, four decisions. Pick the correct hat for each.
A trader thinks crude oil prices will rise and buys a futures contract.
A wheat farmer expects harvest in two months and wants to lock in a sale price today.
A software exporter expects dollar inflows and wants to reduce uncertainty in rupee cash flows.
A trader takes a market position with no existing business exposure, purely to profit from a market view.
The motive decides the label — not the instrument. A hedger and a speculator can both use the same futures contract. What separates them is whether the position is reducing an existing risk, or creating new risk for potential gain.

Activity 3 · Certainty vs profit

Sort each objective into the correct column.

Reduce uncertainty in import cost
Gain from falling stock prices
Make cash flows predictable
Take a bullish market view
Wants certainty
Wants profit from market movement
The hedger's success is not measured by profit on the derivative. It is measured by how much uncertainty has been removed. A hedge can reduce upside along with downside — and that is perfectly acceptable if certainty was the goal.

When students ask only "where is the profit?", they are still thinking like speculators.

The right way to evaluate a hedge: what risk existed before? What uncertainty remains now? How has the total position changed? A hedge that removes uncertainty has done its job — even if the derivative leg alone shows a loss.
5
Activity 5 · Key insight
If you do not hedge, what are you doing?
An exporter expects USD receipts after three months and decides not to hedge the exchange-rate risk. Which statement best describes the situation?
This is one of the deepest ideas in the module. If an exposure already exists, "doing nothing" is not zero risk. It is a choice to remain exposed to whatever the market does. Not hedging is itself a position — even though no derivative is involved.
