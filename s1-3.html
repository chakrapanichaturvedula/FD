Arbitrage &
Trader Hats

The same market event can look completely different depending on why you entered it. This session brings all three trader types into the same moment.

Identify arbitrage as a response to price inconsistency — not prediction
Distinguish arbitrage from speculation using motive, not instrument
Match the same market event to three different trader reactions

Activity 1 · Price inconsistency

The two prices exist at the same time, for the same asset. What is the most important thing to notice?
Arbitrage begins with noticing price inconsistency. The key question is not "will the price rise or fall?" It is "why is the same thing priced differently right now?" That shift in question — from direction to consistency — is the core of arbitrage thinking.
2
Activity 2 · Trader identification
Who reacts immediately to this price gap?
Gold is simultaneously ₹60,000 in Market A and ₹60,600 in Market B. Which trader type moves first?
An arbitrageur does not need a market view. They need: two related markets, a temporary price discrepancy, and the ability to trade both sides quickly. No prediction about direction is needed — the profit comes from the gap itself.

The motive is always the deciding factor. Expectation of price move → speculation. Protection of existing exposure → hedging. Response to price inconsistency across markets → arbitrage. The same type of instrument can appear in all three columns.

Activity 5 · Same event, different hats
A futures price falls sharply. Who sees this as good news?
Nifty futures fall 300 points. For each trader, pick the most likely reaction. The same market move — three different meanings.
Speculator
Was long Nifty futures — entered because she expected the market to rise.
Hedger
Was short futures — entered to protect a long underlying position from a price fall.
Arbitrageur
Entered offsetting positions because the same asset was priced differently — the spread has now closed.
This is one of the deepest ideas in the module. The same market event has no single meaning. Its meaning depends entirely on the trader's motive, their prior exposure, and what they were trying to achieve. The number on the screen is the same for everyone — what it means is completely different.

Arbitrage starts with price inconsistency — not directional prediction

The same market event means different things depending on why you entered
