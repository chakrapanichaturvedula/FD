Underlying
The asset or index on which this futures contract is based. You are not buying Nifty shares directly — you are taking price exposure to the Nifty index through a standardised contract.
NIFTY futures track the Nifty 50 index. If Nifty rises, a long futures position gains.

Expiry date
The date on which this futures contract stops existing and settles. NSE typically has three active contracts: near month, next month, and far month. The nearer the expiry, the higher the trading activity usually.
This is the June contract. A July contract and August contract also trade simultaneously on NSE.

Last traded price (LTP)
The price at which the most recent trade was executed. This is the current market reference price — not necessarily where the next trade will happen. Do not confuse LTP with settlement price.
If two traders just agreed at ₹24,850, that becomes the LTP. The next trade could be higher or lower.

50 shares

Lot size
The minimum quantity in one futures contract. NSE standardises this — you cannot buy "half a contract." Lot size is set by the exchange and revised periodically.
Lot size = 50 means one contract controls 50 units of the underlying index. You cannot trade 30 units — only multiples of 50.

₹12,42,500

Contract value (notional)
The total notional exposure of one futures contract. Formula: Futures price × Lot size. This is NOT the amount you pay upfront — it is the total market exposure you control.
₹24,850 × 50 = ₹12,42,500. You do not pay ₹12,42,500 — you post only a margin deposit to hold this exposure.

Open interest (OI)
The number of futures contracts that are still open and have not been closed. Rising OI means more positions are being created. Falling OI means positions are being closed. This is not the same as volume.
1,82,450 contracts are currently open in this expiry. Each contract has one buyer and one seller who have not yet squared off.

Change in open interest
The percentage change in open interest since the previous session. Rising OI indicates fresh positions being built. Falling OI means existing positions are being closed or squared off.
+4.8% means 4.8% more contracts are open today than yesterday — new participants are entering, not closing positions.

Volume
The number of contracts traded during the current session. Volume tells you how active trading has been today. It is very different from open interest — volume resets to zero every day, while OI accumulates.
3,26,000 contracts changed hands today. But at end of day, OI depends on how many of those were new positions vs closures.

Bid price
The highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay. If you want to sell immediately, you can sell at the bid price — that is the best available buyer right now.
₹24,848 is the highest price any buyer has placed in the order book right now.

Ask price
The lowest price a seller is currently willing to accept. If you want to buy immediately, you pay the ask price. The difference between bid and ask is the bid-ask spread — a measure of transaction cost.
Spread = ₹24,851 − ₹24,848 = ₹3. Tighter spreads mean lower transaction costs and higher liquidity.

Volume
Open Interest
Volume counts contracts traded during the session — it resets to zero each day. Open interest counts contracts that are still alive — it rises with new positions and falls when they are closed. You can have very high volume with near-zero OI if all trades were opened and closed the same day.

Key fields — remember this
Lot size — minimum quantity per contract; set by the exchange
Contract value = futures price × lot size; your total exposure, not what you pay
Open interest — contracts still open; rises with new positions, falls when closed
Volume — contracts traded today; resets daily; not the same as OI
Bid / Ask spread — measures liquidity; narrower is better for traders
