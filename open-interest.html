Buyer A and Seller B are both new — neither held a position before. They just created one futures contract. What happens to open interest?
Open interest counts contracts that are still alive. When two new traders create a contract, one new open position has been added — OI rises. Think of OI as a count of open contracts in the system.

Both traders already held positions. They trade with each other to close their existing positions. What happens to open interest?
When two existing position-holders trade with each other to close, the contract is no longer open. OI falls. OI is the live count of open contracts — when one closes, the count drops.

Activity 4 · The key distinction
100 contracts opened and closed the same day
Scenario
100 futures contracts are opened in the morning. All 100 are squared off by close of trading. What are volume and open interest at end of day?
Think carefully. Volume counts all trades during the day. OI counts contracts still open at end of day.
This is the most important distinction. You can have very high volume with near-zero OI. All 100 contracts traded (so volume ≥ 100) but since all were closed before market close, no open contracts remain — OI = 0. Volume and OI are entirely separate measures.

Activity 5 · Interpretation
What does rising open interest suggest?
Open interest in Nifty futures has been rising steadily for three days. What is the safest basic interpretation?
At this stage, keep the interpretation simple: rising OI = more open positions in the system. Price direction implications from OI come later (as part of market analysis). For now, just understand what OI counts.
Open Interest & Volume — remember this
Open interest = contracts still open; rises when new positions are created, falls when they are closed
Volume = contracts traded during the session; resets to zero each day
High volume + zero OI is possible if all positions opened and closed the same day
Rising OI means more positions are being built in the market
